Unlocking AI’s Potential: Prioritizing the Right Use Cases

GreenGrain Foods Ltd is a mid-sized food manufacturing company experiencing operational inefficiencies and increased competition in the market. They have identified a number of potential AI use cases but currently lack a structured way to evaluate them for delivery.

The Task

Design and apply a prioritisation framework to help GreenGrain Foods decide which AI initiatives to focus on. This solution will be presented to a wide range of audiences with technical abilities.

The Prioritization Matrix

Criteria Description Assessment Metrics Example Score
Business Value/Impact Evaluates potential for revenue growth, cost reductions, market differentiation, and enhanced customer experience. Estimated ROI (%), Potential Cost Savings (£), Market Reach & Differentiation. Score (1-5) 5
Strategic Alignment Assesses how well the initiative supports the company’s long-term vision and competitive positioning. Alignment Score, supports key business objectives (Yes/No) & Competitive Edge Factor. Overall Score (1-5) 4
Technical Feasibility Considers implementation complexity, resource availability, and integration with existing systems. Estimated Development Effort (Hours), Complexity Rating (1-5) & Compatibility with Existing Systems (%). Overall Score (1-5) 3
Data Readiness & Quality Reviews whether the necessary data is available, clean, and ready to power the AI solution. Data Availability Score (1-5), Clean Dataset Percentage (%), Completeness of Historical Data (%). Overall Score (1-5) 4
Cost & Risk Assessment Analyzes expected costs, ongoing expenditures, and potential risks, including regulatory and scalability concerns. Estimated Implementation Cost (£), Risk Exposure (1-5) & Regulatory compliance hurdles (Yes/No). Overall Score (1-5) 2
Time-to-Value Estimates the deployment timeline and the time needed to achieve measurable benefits. Expected rollout time (Weeks), Time before measurable impact (Months) & Urgency rating (1-5). Overall Score (1-5) 3

Formula for RICE Style Prioritization Score

Click to expand the prioritization formula

We define an AI prioritization score based on weighted criteria:

Priority Score Formula:

Priority Score = V × A × F × D C × T

Where:

  • V = Business Value (Revenue growth, cost savings, market positioning)
  • A = Strategic Alignment (Vision alignment, competitive advantage)
  • F = Feasibility (Development complexity, integration readiness)
  • D = Data Readiness (Data availability, quality)
  • C = Cost & Risk (Financial investment, regulatory concerns)
  • T = Time-to-Value (Deployment speed, measurable impact timeframe)

Scoring Breakdown (1-5 Scale):

  • Higher scores indicate greater feasibility, alignment, and value.
  • Lower cost & time increase prioritization.
  • Dividing by Cost & Time favors high-impact, low-effort AI initiatives.

Example Use Case Calculation:

Suppose an AI project scores:

  • V = 5, A = 4, F = 3, D = 4
  • C = 2, T = 3

Priority Score = 5 × 4 × 3 × 4 2 × 3 = 240 6 = 40

A higher score suggests strong impact potential with manageable effort.

Visualizing the Prioritization Framework

📌 AI Matrix Explanation: Maps AI initiatives based on feasibility and cost.


# Load required libraries
library(ggplot2)

# Define prioritization criteria with feasibility and cost scores
eisenhower_data <- data.frame(
  Criteria = c("Business Value", "Strategic Alignment", "Technical Feasibility", 
               "Data Readiness", "Cost & Risk", "Time-to-Value"),
  Feasibility = c(5, 4, 3, 4, 2, 4),
  Cost = c(2, 3, 4, 2.5, 5, 1)
)

Eisenhower Matrix Summary:

This matrix categorizes AI initiatives based on Feasibility and Cost, assigning them to four quadrants:

  • Quick Wins (High Feasibility, Low Cost): Business Value and Time-to-Value fall here, meaning they should be prioritized for immediate action.
  • Strategic Investments (High Feasibility, High Cost): Strategic Alignment and Data Readiness are positioned here—worthy long-term investments, despite their higher cost.
  • Rethink (Low Feasibility, Low Cost): Technical Feasibility sits in this area, indicating the need for further refinement before implementation.
  • Avoid (Low Feasibility, High Cost): Cost & Risk lands here, suggesting it's too expensive and difficult to justify implementation.

This visualization is useful for balancing resources effectively and ensuring high-value, achievable initiatives get priority.

📊 Correlation Analysis: Examines statistical relationships between feasibility, cost, and prioritization factors.


# Load library
library(corrplot)

# Compute correlation matrix
cor_matrix <- cor(eisenhower_data)

# Generate a more blended correlation plot
corrplot(
  cor_matrix, method = "shade", 
  col = colorRampPalette(c("darkblue", "white", "darkred"))(200),
  addCoef.col = "black",
  tl.cex = 1.2,
  cl.pos = "b",
  number.cex = 0.8,
  shade.col = NA, # Allows colors to blend
  bg = "white", # Clean background for diffusion effect
  mar = c(0, 0, 1, 0) # Adjust margins
)

Correlation Plot Summary:

This correlation matrix reveals relationships between key prioritization criteria:

  • Strong Positive Correlations: Business Value aligns well with Strategic Alignment and Data Readiness, meaning investments in these areas often reinforce each other.
  • Negative or Weak Relationships: Cost & Risk has weaker connections with other criteria, reinforcing its high cost trade-off.
  • Technical Feasibility Links: Technical Feasibility shows moderate relationships across multiple factors, meaning it plays a bridging role in viability analysis.

Using this correlation visualization, we can assess dependencies and make evidence-based prioritization decisions.

Key Insights & Takeaways

  • High Feasibility & Low Cost → Ideal for Quick Wins.
  • High Feasibility & High Cost → Strategic, but needs investment.
  • Cost & Business Value show inverse correlation → Not all costly projects drive strong value.
  • Feasibility strongly links to Strategic Alignment → Well-aligned initiatives tend to be easier to execute.

💡 This framework enables transparent, evidence-based AI decision-making, evolving dynamically with market shifts and data availability.
